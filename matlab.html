Platonic Solid Truncation

Truncation is any operation on a polytope in which veritces are replaced with faces. Particular depths of truncation will result in distinct symmetries.

In order of depth, the polyhedral operations which are a subset of trunction are uniform truncation, retification, bitruncation, and dual. The term 'trunction' typically implies 'uniform truncation'.

These operations could be visualized by inclosing the polytope within its own dual. As the dual shrinks in size, the enclosed polytope being is truncated to various depths until it becomes the enclosing dual.

The images show the truncation process of the Platonic Solids although truncation could be done on any polytope.

The cube duals the octahedron

The icosohedron duals the dodecahedron

The tetrahedron is its own upside down dual!

Time Dependent Schrodinger Equation

In quantum mechanics, the wave function is a mathematical representation of a physical system. The wave function, represented by ${\Psi(\mathbf{r}, t)}$ , contains all the information of a system's state. The function value of wave functions are probability amplitudes for position, but information about other physical properties could be extracted by applying specific operators.

To be a valid wave function, ${\Psi(\mathbf{r}, t)}$ must satisfy the Time Dependant Schrodinger Equation (TDSE). This is an equation describing the time evolution of a wave function in a certain potential. This equation was derived from a combination of quantum and classical physic principles.

${i\hbar \frac{\partial}{\partial t} \Psi(\mathbf{r}, t) = -\frac{\hbar^2}{2m} \nabla^2 \Psi(\mathbf{r}, t) + V(\mathbf{r}, t) \Psi(\mathbf{r}, t)}$

Solving for ${\frac{\partial}{\partial t} \Psi(\mathbf{r}, t)}$:

${\frac{\partial}{\partial t} \Psi(\mathbf{r}, t) = \frac{i\hbar}{2m} \nabla^2 \Psi(\mathbf{r}, t) - V(\mathbf{r}, t) \Psi(\mathbf{r}, t)\frac{i}{\hbar}}$

Given this requirement for ${\frac{\partial}{\partial t} \Psi(\mathbf{r}, t)}$ , and an initial wave function at ${t=0}$. The Runge Kutta method could be used to determine the time evolution of the wave function within a given potential ${V(\mathbf{r}, t)}$.

Since ${\Psi(\mathbf{r}, t)}$ conveys probability ampltiudes, the probability distrubtion function (PDF) for particle detection at a given location is calculated by ${\Psi^*(\mathbf{r}, t) \cdot \Psi(\mathbf{r}, t)=real(\Psi(\mathbf{r}, t))^2+imag(\Psi(\mathbf{r}, t))^2}$

The TDSE demonstrates the quantum uncertainty principle which states that the uncertainty in position is inversely proportionaly to the uncertainity of momentum. The exact relationship is expressed by the following equation ${\Delta x \Delta p \geq \frac{\hbar}{2}}$. By decreasing the initial width (decreasing positional uncertainty) of the guassian envelope, the momentum of the wave function becomes more uncertain. The movement of the wavefunction becomes less defined.

In quantum mechanics the momentum, is directly related to the wavenumbers of the wave. The higher the wave number, the more momentum and more energy the system contains. The higher the wave number, the more distance the wave could travel away from the origin before being reflected by the potential.

Robot Manipulator Trajectory

This program defines 5 paths which join to make 1 looping path. The joint variables needed for the given manipulator to traverse the path are calculated using the inverse kinematic equations. The joint variables are then verified using the foward kinematic transformation and A matrices. The actual path and orientation calculated is plotted and animated over the desired path.

The positioning of the paths was calculated using trigonometric analysis and the orientations were calculated using rotation matrices. To help debug the code and check these positions/orientations, the T matrix of the manipulator was referenced.
